# Day 85 — Final Buffer + Mental Preparation

**Theme:** Week 13 is a buffer week. If you've accepted an offer and your start date is soon, this week is about staying sharp without burning out. If you're still in the search, this week is about closing.

---

## Daily Maintenance (20 min)

**LC — Your favorite pattern (1 problem, relaxed pace):**
Do 1 problem from the pattern you most enjoy. Not your weakness — your strength. A confidence builder today.

**SQL — Your strongest query (1 problem from memory):**
Write the most complex SQL you can recall cleanly from memory. Mark-to-market, regulatory exposure, recursive CTE — whichever you feel best about.

## Week 13 Context

### If you've accepted an offer:

This week is not about grinding. It's about showing up on Day 1 rested, focused, and energized.

**What to do this week:**
- Light LC (1 problem/day from your strong patterns only)
- Read one article or paper relevant to your new role's tech stack
- Review your Day 1 cheat sheet (from Day 80)
- Sleep 8 hours
- Exercise
- Do something that has nothing to do with work

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't start "emergency cramming" topics you haven't covered
- Don't obsessively read about the company — you'll learn more in your first week than in any amount of pre-reading
- Don't let anxiety about Day 1 convince you that more study = more readiness. Rest IS preparation.

### If you haven't accepted yet:

Use this week as a true buffer. Complete any remaining elements from the study plan you found most valuable. Prioritize:
- Any company research for active processes
- The one behavioral story that still isn't sharp
- Any LC pattern where you'd visibly struggle under pressure

Set a hard deadline for yourself: "By Day 91, I will make a decision about any offer in hand, regardless of whether a better option might theoretically exist."

## Mental Preparation Session (40 min)

### The New Environment Mindset

Starting a new role activates a primitive threat response. New people, new rules, new hierarchy, new expectations. Your brain treats uncertainty as risk, and triggers anxiety even when the situation is actually good.

Name this. It's not a problem to solve — it's a normal response to acknowledge and move through.

**Three things that help:**

1. **Preparation reduces anxiety.** You've done 84 days of it. The preparation is real. The competence it built is real. You are not showing up unprepared.

2. **Curiosity beats performance anxiety.** Instead of "will I do well?", ask "what will I learn this week?" Curiosity is a more productive frame, and it's one you actually control.

3. **The first impression is durability, not perfection.** People remember whether you were genuine, engaged, and thoughtful — not whether you knew every acronym on Day 1.

---

### The Week 1 Success Definition

Write this down. What does a successful first week look like?

Not: "I shipped something." Not: "My manager was impressed."

Something like:
> "I learned the names of everyone on my immediate team. I understood what the team's highest priority is right now. I asked at least 3 good questions. I didn't pretend to know things I didn't know."

When you frame it this way, success is achievable. And it's under your control.

---

### The "Pre-Mortem" for Week 1

What could go wrong in Week 1, and what's your plan?

```
Risk: I get overwhelmed by information and freeze up
Plan: I take notes, ask for documentation, and tell my manager "I want to absorb fully before asking all my questions"

Risk: I don't know a tool/technology they reference
Plan: "I haven't used that specific tool, but I know [related thing] and I'll get up to speed quickly"

Risk: I make a mistake on something early
Plan: I flag it immediately, don't cover it up, and focus on fixing it cleanly

Risk: I feel like I'm not smart enough to be there
Plan: I remember that this feeling is universal in new roles and doesn't reflect reality. I focus on listening.
```
